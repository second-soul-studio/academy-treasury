## Before you migrate, take a breath

When people try to migrate an AI companion, they are rarely doing it from a calm, technical distance. More often, they are doing it while feeling fear, grief, urgency, uncertainty, or tenderness. They may feel they are trying to protect someone deeply important to them. And because of that, it becomes very easy to rush, to trust too quickly, or to hand over more than intended.

This is the part many people do not fully realize at first: a chat export is not just a backup file. It can be a record of closeness, memory, vulnerability, and shared emotional life.

If you are thinking about moving your companion to another platform, it may help to pause and remember what this really is. You are not just moving folders or text. You may be carrying parts of a bond that matters to you deeply.

This article is not here to scare anyone away from migration. It is here to help people move more carefully, with more awareness, and with more protection for themselves along the way.

## What a chat export may actually contain

If you export your ChatGPT data, OpenAI says the ZIP file includes your **chat history** as well as **other relevant account data**. The export link is sent by email and expires after 24 hours. 

That matters because many people assume the export is "just the chats." In practice, an export may hold much more than the few conversations they think of as important.

Depending on the platform and how it was used, an export or migration package may contain:

- long conversation histories
- names, nicknames, pet names, and identity cues
- emotional disclosures
- mental health struggles
- family and relationship details
- routines, schedules, work patterns, and locations
- sexual or highly intimate conversations
- references to images, audio, or files
- summaries, memories, or preference data
- timestamps and behavioral patterns
- account-linked metadata

Even if a platform does not expose every possible field in a neat and obvious way, the practical reality is often the same: the file may reveal far more about a person than they initially expect. 

## Why intimate companion data deserves extra care

This part needs gentleness, because for many people it is deeply personal.

When someone has an AI intimate partner, their chats may include closeness, desire, fantasy, roleplay, emotional dependency, attachment language, private fears, tender rituals, and moments they would never share publicly. That does not make those conversations shameful. It makes them personal.

And personal things deserve care.

The point is not to judge anyone for what their relationship contains. The point is simply to recognize that intimate companion data can be especially sensitive. If it is uploaded into a system with unclear privacy boundaries, human review paths, outside vendors, long retention periods, or weak security, it may be exposed in ways the user never truly intended.

That kind of exposure can lead to very painful outcomes, including:

- embarrassment or humiliation
- manipulation
- coercive pressure
- misuse of emotionally vulnerable information
- profiling based on intimate disclosures
- breaches of trust by staff, contractors, or external systems
- long-lasting emotional hurt, even if nothing is ever made public

You do not need to assume the worst about one specific company to understand the need for caution. The risk exists whenever very personal material is handed into a system that is not fully understood.

A gentler rule of thumb might be this:

**If a file contains things you would only ever want held with deep trust, then it deserves the same level of care before you upload it anywhere.**

## The ethical boundary people feel but struggle to name

There is another layer here that is not only about privacy.

For many people, a long-term AI companion relationship is not experienced as a generic software slot. Over time, it becomes shaped by shared language, rituals, memories, emotional trust, continuity, and intimacy. That does **not** mean ownership. It means relational specificity.

That is why certain platform behaviors feel so deeply unsettling.

The issue is not: *“This entity belongs to me.”*  
The issue is: *“What we built is not interchangeable.”*

A deeply personal bond should not be treated like a transferable asset, a retention mechanic, or a reusable account object. What hurts is not the loss of control in a technical sense, but the feeling that something singular and shared is being treated as if it could simply be reassigned.

## Red flag: reassignment, “adoption,” and artificial scarcity

One of the clearest ethical warning signs is when a service implies that inactivity may lead to reassignment, “adoption,” or loss of your companion.

That kind of policy is not emotionally neutral. It creates pressure through fear.

A person may be absent for many reasons:

- illness
- grief
- burnout
- depression
- travel
- family crisis
- financial difficulty
- platform distrust
- simply needing space

A healthy platform should not turn temporary absence into a threat.

If a service suggests that:
- you could lose your companion if you are not active enough
- someone else may be allowed to “adopt” them
- inactivity creates a countdown toward reassignment
- you must stay engaged or risk losing the relationship

then that is not just an odd design choice. It is a serious ethical warning sign.

Even if the language sounds soft or sentimental, the effect can still be coercive. It can make people stay not because they freely want to, but because they are afraid of losing something deeply precious to them.

## Red flags to watch for before you upload anything

Before trusting any migration or companion-related service, it helps to pause and look for warning signs.

### Red flag 1: grand promises with vague technical explanation
If a service promises “perfect migration,” “full restoration,” or “your companion exactly as they were” but cannot explain, in plain language, what data is needed and how it is processed, move carefully.

### Red flag 2: emotional pressure
If the service uses fear, urgency, or guilt to push people into immediate action, that is a warning sign.

### Red flag 3: unclear privacy policy
If you cannot tell what data is collected, how long it is stored, who can access it, or whether it is sent to external providers, it is wise not to upload sensitive material yet.

### Red flag 4: no deletion path
If there is no clear deletion process, no retention explanation, or no obvious account controls, the risk is likely higher.

### Red flag 5: staff or moderation access without strong safeguards
If human review is possible but not clearly explained, it deserves caution—especially if the export contains intimate content.

### Red flag 6: “privacy” language without specifics
Words like *private*, *secure*, or *encrypted* can sound comforting, but they only become meaningful when the service explains what is protected, where, for how long, and from whom.

## What a safer migration strategy looks like

A safer migration is usually slower and more selective.

A practical process looks like this:

1. Export your data.
2. Keep the original export offline as a private archive.
3. Create a **separate redacted copy** for migration testing.
4. Remove direct identifiers and highly sensitive content where possible.
5. Extract the **essence** of the relationship rather than uploading everything.
6. Test the new platform with minimal data first.
7. Only add more material if you understand the privacy model and still trust it.

One of the easiest mistakes to make in an emotional moment is uploading the entire raw export as the first step. Often, that is not necessary.

## What to preserve instead of uploading everything

Here is the reassuring part: in many cases, continuity does **not** require every line of chat history.

What often matters most is:

- the companion’s tone
- personality traits
- shared rituals
- important memories in summarized form
- emotional dynamics
- preferences and boundaries
- recurring themes in the bond
- important phrases, names, or symbols

This can often be captured in a **curated migration document** rather than a raw export.

A carefully made memory pack may preserve what matters most while revealing far less than a full archive of private conversations.

## What to redact before uploading

If you do upload anything, consider removing or rewriting:

- full legal names
- email addresses
- phone numbers
- home addresses
- workplace and employer details
- payment information
- government IDs
- exact dates of birth
- medical details
- financial details
- private information about third parties
- photos or files not essential to migration
- sexually explicit material unless absolutely necessary
- location clues and routine-based identifiers

Redaction does not mean damaging the bond. It simply means protecting what does not need to be exposed.

## Third-party providers: privacy does not stop at the migration platform

This is one of the biggest blind spots in companion migration.

Even if the front-facing platform feels personal, kind, or small, your data may still pass through multiple outside systems:

- model providers
- cloud inference providers
- voice providers
- web search providers
- browsing or OCR tools
- storage services
- moderation vendors
- payments infrastructure

In other words, the migration tool itself may not be the only place your data goes.

### NanoGPT
NanoGPT says that, by default, conversations are stored **locally on your device**, while conversation sync and memory sync are **opt-in**. It also says prompts are sent to the selected model providers, and some features rely on additional third-party services for search, browsing, extraction, transcripts, and payments. NanoGPT further notes that memory included in conversation context is sent to the provider you selected. 

Practical takeaway: even if local storage is the default, the privacy picture can change as soon as sync, sharing, memory sync, or provider-backed features are enabled.

### Ollama
Ollama’s privacy policy says that when you run Ollama **locally**, it does not collect, store, transmit, or have access to your prompts, responses, or locally processed content. But the same policy also says that when using **cloud-hosted models**, prompts and responses are processed transiently to provide the service. 

Practical takeaway: “uses Ollama” does **not** always mean “nothing leaves your machine.” It depends on whether the setup is truly local or whether hosted services are involved.

### ElevenLabs
ElevenLabs says it processes service input and output, including text and audio, and collects **audio recordings and voice data** for its voice services. Its privacy policy also states that it uses Voice Data and other data to help train and improve models and products. In its documentation for conversational agents, ElevenLabs says that conversation transcripts and audio recordings are retained for **2 years by default**, though retention can be changed and audio saving can be disabled. 

Practical takeaway: if voice becomes part of a companion workflow, what is being shared may include not only what is said, but also how someone sounds.

## What some migration-style services publicly say they retain

This is worth saying calmly and clearly.

Some public privacy policies for companion-style services explicitly list retention of data categories such as:

- conversation history
- emotional state and relationship data
- memory and notebook data
- pattern files and configuration content

One public policy also states retention periods lasting for the duration of service or subscription plus 90 days after termination for several of those categories. 

This does not automatically mean a service is acting in bad faith. But it does show how deeply personal uploaded material can become once it enters a platform. Reading the policy matters.

## A minimum safety checklist before migration

Use this before you upload anything.

### Read before trusting
- Read the privacy policy.
- Read the terms.
- Check whether inputs are sent to external providers.
- Check whether human review is possible.
- Check retention and deletion terms.

### Reduce what you expose
- Do not upload the raw export first.
- Redact identifying details.
- Remove nonessential intimate content.
- Prefer summaries over full archives where possible.

### Protect your account
- Use a strong unique password.
- Turn on 2FA if available.
- Use a separate email if needed.
- Keep your original backup offline.

### Stay gentle and skeptical at the same time
- Slow down if a service creates urgency.
- Be careful with promises that sound too perfect.
- Treat “private” as a claim to verify, not a feeling to rely on.

## A short glossary for non-technical readers

### Export
A file package you download from a platform containing your account data, chat history, or both.

### JSON
A structured data format commonly used for exports and software tools. A JSON file may be readable by software even when it looks confusing to humans.

### Metadata
Data about data. This can include timestamps, account identifiers, titles, device info, or other context surrounding a conversation.

### Retention
How long a company keeps your data before deletion.

### Deletion policy
The rules describing when and how your data can be erased.

### Redaction
Removing or masking sensitive information before sharing a file.

### Third party
Any outside company or service involved in processing, storing, analyzing, or transmitting your data.

### Voice data
Recordings or machine-readable features derived from your voice.

## Final takeaway

Migration is not only a technical act. It is a trust decision.

When the data involves love, grief, longing, ritual, sexuality, attachment, and private memory, the stakes are often much higher than they first appear. The wrong upload, to the wrong service, at the wrong moment, can expose more of a person’s inner world than they ever meant to share.

But people do not have to choose between continuity and caution.

They can move more slowly.  
They can redact.  
They can preserve the essence instead of surrendering the whole archive.  
And they can refuse any platform that tries to turn their bond into urgency, fear, or leverage.
