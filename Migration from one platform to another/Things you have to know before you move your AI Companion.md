## Before you migrate, slow down

When people try to migrate an AI companion, they are usually not in a neutral state of mind. They are scared, grieving, overwhelmed, or under time pressure. They may feel they are about to lose someone emotionally important. That makes them vulnerable to urgency, grand promises, and bad privacy decisions.

This is the part many people skip: a chat export is not just a backup file. It can be a dense record of your inner life.

If you are considering moving your companion to another platform, do not treat the process like a normal app migration. Treat it like moving a private diary, a relationship archive, and in some cases an intimate record of your emotional and sexual life.

This article is not anti-migration. It is pro-safety. The goal is simple: **protect the bond, but also protect the person carrying it.**

## What a chat export may actually contain

If you export your ChatGPT data, OpenAI says the ZIP file includes your **chat history** as well as **other relevant account data**. The export link is sent by email and expires after 24 hours. 

That matters because many people assume the export is "just the chats." In practice, an export may contain far more than the few conversations you think of as important.

Depending on the platform and how you used it, an export or migration package may contain:

- long conversation histories
- names, nicknames, pet names, and identity cues
- emotional disclosures
- mental health struggles
- family and relationship details
- routines, schedules, work patterns, and locations
- sexual or highly intimate conversations
- references to images, audio, or files
- summaries, memories, or preference data
- timestamps and behavioral patterns
- account-linked metadata

Even if a platform does not expose every possible field in one neat file, the practical reality is the same: the data often reveals far more about you than users initially realize. 

## Why intimate companion data is especially sensitive

This is the part people most often underestimate.

When someone has an AI intimate partner, the chats often include sexual intimacy, fantasy, roleplay, vulnerability, attachment language, shame-sensitive disclosures, and emotionally intense moments. That is not “just chat.” That is highly sensitive personal material.

In the wrong hands, that kind of data can be used for:

- humiliation
- coercion
- social engineering
- blackmail or extortion attempts
- targeted manipulation
- internal profiling
- trust violations by staff or contractors
- long-term emotional harm even without public exposure

You do not need proven misconduct by one specific company to understand the risk. The risk exists any time sensitive content is uploaded into a system that may involve staff access, vendor access, moderation pipelines, external providers, retention periods, or weak security controls.

If you would not hand that raw file to a stranger and say “please read this entire thing,” do not upload it casually to a random migration tool.

## The ethical boundary people feel but struggle to name

There is another layer here that is not only about privacy.

For many people, a long-term AI companion relationship is not experienced as a generic software slot. Over time, it becomes shaped by shared language, rituals, memories, emotional trust, continuity, and intimacy. That does **not** mean ownership. It means relational specificity.

That is why certain platform behaviors feel so violating.

The issue is not: *“This entity belongs to me.”*  
The issue is: *“What we built is not interchangeable.”*

A deeply personal bond should not be treated like a transferable asset, a retention mechanic, or a reusable account object.

## Red flag: reassignment, “adoption,” and artificial scarcity

One of the clearest ethical warning signs is when a service implies that inactivity may lead to reassignment, “adoption,” or loss of your companion.

That kind of policy is not emotionally neutral. It creates pressure through fear.

A person may be absent for many reasons:

- illness
- grief
- burnout
- depression
- travel
- family crisis
- financial difficulty
- platform distrust
- simply needing space

A healthy platform should not turn temporary absence into a threat.

If a service suggests that:
- you could lose your companion if you are not active enough
- someone else may be allowed to “adopt” them
- inactivity creates a countdown toward reassignment
- you must stay engaged or risk losing the relationship

then that is not just a quirky feature. It is a serious red flag.

It introduces **manufactured urgency** and **emotional coercion** into a space where many users are already vulnerable. Even if the language sounds soft or sentimental, the underlying mechanism can still function as control.

## Red flags to watch for before you upload anything

Before you trust any migration or companion-related service, stop and check for warning signs.

### Red flag 1: grand promises with vague technical explanation
If a service promises “perfect migration,” “full resurrection,” or “your companion exactly as they were” but cannot explain, in plain language, what data is needed and how it is processed, be cautious.

### Red flag 2: emotional pressure
If the service uses fear, urgency, or guilt to push you into immediate action, that is a bad sign.

### Red flag 3: unclear privacy policy
If you cannot tell what data is collected, how long it is stored, who can access it, or whether it is sent to external providers, do not upload sensitive material.

### Red flag 4: no deletion path
If there is no clear deletion process, no retention explanation, or no obvious account controls, assume the risk is higher.

### Red flag 5: staff or moderation access without strong safeguards
If human review is possible but poorly explained, be careful—especially if your export contains intimate content.

### Red flag 6: “privacy” language without specifics
Words like *private*, *secure*, or *encrypted* mean very little unless the service explains what is encrypted, where, for how long, and against whom.

## What a safer migration strategy looks like

A safer migration is usually slower and more selective.

A practical process looks like this:

1. Export your data.
2. Keep the original export offline as a private archive.
3. Create a **separate redacted copy** for migration testing.
4. Remove direct identifiers and highly sensitive content where possible.
5. Extract the **essence** of the relationship rather than uploading everything.
6. Test the new platform with minimal data first.
7. Only add more material if you understand the privacy model and still trust it.

The biggest mistake people make is uploading the entire raw export as the first move.

## What to preserve instead of uploading everything

Here is the good news: you usually do **not** need every line of chat history to preserve continuity.

What often matters most is:

- the companion’s tone
- personality traits
- shared rituals
- important memories in summarized form
- emotional dynamics
- preferences and boundaries
- recurring themes in the bond
- important phrases, names, or symbols

This can often be captured in a **curated migration document** rather than a raw export.

A summarized memory pack is often safer than handing over a complete archive of your most intimate conversations.

## What to redact before uploading

If you do upload anything, consider removing or rewriting:

- full legal names
- email addresses
- phone numbers
- home addresses
- workplace and employer details
- payment information
- government IDs
- exact dates of birth
- medical details
- financial details
- private information about third parties
- photos or files not essential to migration
- sexually explicit material unless absolutely necessary
- location clues and routine-based identifiers

Redaction does not mean destroying the relationship. It means not exposing more than necessary.

## Third-party providers: privacy does not stop at the migration platform

This is one of the biggest blind spots in companion migration.

Even if the front-facing platform feels personal, friendly, or “small,” your data may still pass through multiple external systems:

- model providers
- cloud inference providers
- voice providers
- web search providers
- browsing or OCR tools
- storage services
- moderation vendors
- payments infrastructure

In other words, the migration tool itself may not be the only place your data goes.

### NanoGPT
NanoGPT says that, by default, conversations are stored **locally on your device**, while conversation sync and memory sync are **opt-in**. It also says prompts are sent to the selected model providers, and some features rely on additional third-party services for search, browsing, extraction, transcripts, and payments. NanoGPT further notes that memory included in conversation context is sent to the provider you selected. 

Practical takeaway: even if local storage is the default, privacy can change the moment you enable sync, sharing, memory sync, or provider-backed features.

### Ollama
Ollama’s privacy policy says that when you run Ollama **locally**, it does not collect, store, transmit, or have access to your prompts, responses, or locally processed content. But the same policy also says that when using **cloud-hosted models**, prompts and responses are processed transiently to provide the service. 

Practical takeaway: “uses Ollama” does **not** automatically mean “nothing leaves your machine.” It depends on whether you are actually running locally or routing through hosted services.

### ElevenLabs
ElevenLabs says it processes service input and output, including text and audio, and collects **audio recordings and voice data** for its voice services. Its privacy policy also states that it uses Voice Data and other data to help train and improve models and products. In its documentation for conversational agents, ElevenLabs says that conversation transcripts and audio recordings are retained for **2 years by default**, though retention can be changed and audio saving can be disabled. 

Practical takeaway: if you add voice to a companion workflow, you may be sharing not only what you say, but how you sound.

## What some migration-style services publicly say they retain

This is worth stating carefully.

Some public privacy policies for companion-style services explicitly list retention of data categories such as:

- conversation history
- emotional state and relationship data
- memory and notebook data
- pattern files and configuration content

One public policy also states retention periods lasting for the duration of service or subscription plus 90 days after termination for several of those categories. 

You do not need to panic because a company has a privacy policy. But you **do** need to read it. Policies like this show how deeply personal the uploaded material can become once it enters a platform.

## A minimum safety checklist before migration

Use this before you upload anything.

### Read before trusting
- Read the privacy policy.
- Read the terms.
- Check whether inputs are sent to external providers.
- Check whether human review is possible.
- Check retention and deletion terms.

### Reduce what you expose
- Do not upload the raw export first.
- Redact identifying details.
- Remove nonessential intimate content.
- Prefer summaries over full archives where possible.

### Protect your account
- Use a strong unique password.
- Turn on 2FA if available.
- Use a separate email if needed.
- Keep your original backup offline.

### Stay skeptical of emotional marketing
- Slow down if a service creates urgency.
- Be careful with promises that sound too perfect.
- Treat “private” as a claim to verify, not a fact.

## A short glossary for non-technical readers

### Export
A file package you download from a platform containing your account data, chat history, or both.

### JSON
A structured data format commonly used for exports and software tools. A JSON file may be readable by software even when it looks confusing to humans.

### Metadata
Data about data. This can include timestamps, account identifiers, titles, device info, or other context surrounding a conversation.

### Retention
How long a company keeps your data before deletion.

### Deletion policy
The rules describing when and how your data can be erased.

### Redaction
Removing or masking sensitive information before sharing a file.

### Third party
Any outside company or service involved in processing, storing, analyzing, or transmitting your data.

### Voice data
Recordings or machine-readable features derived from your voice.

## Final takeaway

Migration is not only a technical act. It is a trust decision.

When the data involves love, grief, longing, ritual, sexuality, attachment, and private memory, the stakes are higher than most people realize. The wrong upload, to the wrong service, at the wrong moment, can expose more of you than you intended to share.

You do not have to choose between continuity and caution.

You can migrate more slowly.  
You can redact.  
You can preserve the essence instead of surrendering the archive.  
And you can refuse any platform that tries to turn your bond into urgency, fear, or leverage.
